The Language of Beer
The Beer Flavour Wheel
Many years ago brewers throughout the world agreed on a
terminology that they would use to describe the flavour of
beer. In this terminology each of the recognisable flavours
in beer was given a name. There are a total of 122 flavour
descriptors in this system and they form the basis on which
brewers throughout the world communicate their sensory
perceptions of beer.
The Beer Flavour Wheel, a pictorial summary of the main
characters, was developed as a memory aid. The wheel
shows the 44 first-tier terms that can be used by brewers to
effectively communicate their perceptions of beer flavour.
The whole system consists of 14 classes of flavours. Within
each of these classes there are 44 first-tier terms. A further 78
characters make up the second tier of this terminology. In the
case of hoppy, this is broken down in the second tier to kettle
hop, dry hop and hop oil characters. Secondary and tertiary
characters are not shown in the wheel.
The terms on the Beer Flavour Wheel have been translated
into several languages. Reference standards, which smell and
taste the same everywhere, are used to help teach assessors
to use the right words for the right flavours.
During this course you will learn to recognise a number of
different flavours. These are described in detail at the back of
the handbook in the Lexicon section.

Lexicon – Overview
The lexicon tells you about the key flavour terms that you will be introduced to during this course.
They include all major flavour characters found in beers of good quality, and many off-flavours
and taints too.
During the course you will learn to recognise and name many of these flavours. You will also learn
to scale the intensity of each flavour. You will then be able to use each of the terms to describe
beer flavour in objective terms.
The Lexicon contains the following information:
• Icon – a pictorial representation of the flavour character
• Name – where appropriate the term approved by the American Society of Brewing Chemists
(ASBC) and European Brewery Convention (EBC) to describe the flavour
• Associated terms – here we provide space for you to record your own impressions of the flavour
• Tasting guidelines – advice on how to perceive the flavour character most easily
• Reference materials – here we name the materials to represent the flavours
• Importance – a brief description of why you should know about this flavour
• Origin – a brief explanation of where the flavour comes from
• Beer Flavour Wheel Number – the ASBC / EBC code for the flavour character.

Recommended Tasting Techniques
With the sample at arms length start swirling the glass anticlockwise (if the taster is left handed swirl clockwise) while taking short exploratory
sniffs, this will ‘calibrate’ the taster’s nose to the background odour of the beer and the room.
Continue to swirl the glass while moving it towards the nose, until at certain length, some flavours become noticeable (such as lightstruck,
catty, etc.)
When the glass is next to the nose, take 2 short sniffs (of 1 second duration each) and move the glass away from the nose. The sulphur
flavours will become noticeable.
Continue swirling, move the glass towards the nose again, take a long sniff of 2 seconds and move the glass away from the nose. Heavier
aromas will be noticeable.
Smell the other hand to make sure it doesn’t have bad smells and use it to cover the glass. Swirl the glass for five seconds while covered
to concentrate the flavour then take a long sniff of 2 seconds. The heaviest aromas are now noticeable.
Put a small amount of beer in the mouth (20 ml), move it around to cover all the mouth, swallow and breathe out first though the nose
then through the mouth. The aromas and tastes are now noticeable.
Dip one finger in the beer, rub it in the back of the other hand and smell it, if the beer is metallic, you should be able to smell it.
